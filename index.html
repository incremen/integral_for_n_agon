The Integral n-gon

A regular polygon emerges from pure numerical integration — no geometry tricks.

Controls

The Equation

$$z(X) = \int_0^X \exp\!\left(i \cdot \frac{2\pi}{n} \cdot \sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\theta(x-k)}}\right) dx$$

How It Works

Parametric Complex Curve

This isn't a standard $y = f(x)$ graph. The integral outputs a complex number $z = a + bi$ as the variable $x$ (acting as "time") increases. We plot the real part on the horizontal axis and the imaginary part on the vertical. The curve can loop back on itself because $x$ drives the position, not an axis.

Constant Drawing Speed

The integrand is $e^{i \cdot \phi(x)}$, which always has magnitude 1. The "pen" moves at a constant velocity — it can only change direction, never speed up or slow down. This is arc-length parameterization: the shape's perimeter equals the integration range.

The Steering Wheel: Sum of Sigmoids

The phase $\phi(x) = \frac{2\pi}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\sigma(\theta(x-k))$ steers the pen. Each sigmoid activates as $x$ passes the integer $k$, making the sum act like a smooth staircase. Every step-up of 1 rotates the pen by $\frac{2\pi}{n}$, creating one corner of the polygon.

Sharpness ($\theta$) and Curvature

$\theta$ controls how abruptly each sigmoid switches from 0 to 1. Low $\theta$ means a slow transition — the angle changes gradually, producing rounded corners. High $\theta$ makes the switch nearly instantaneous, producing sharp corners. Crank it up to 300 and the integral essentially becomes a discrete sum of straight-line vectors.

Closing the Loop

Because the sum has $n$ terms, the total phase change after $x$ passes $n$ is $\frac{2\pi}{n} \cdot n = 2\pi$ — a full 360° rotation. This returns the pen to its original heading, closing the polygon perfectly.

Numerical Integration

There's no closed-form solution, so we use Euler's method: start at the origin, advance $x$ by a tiny $dx$, evaluate the integrand, and accumulate the result. The polygon emerges entirely from this blind vector arithmetic — no vertices are calculated, no geometry is hardcoded.
